Sales enablement application

IBM B2B progressive web app

IBM
Aug 2016 - Oct 2017
Agile/Fullstack

This sales enablement application was designed and built from scratch to test our client’s hypothesis that IBM sellers could be better activated through a digital tool. Her vision was to create an app that could help sellers collaboratively identify quality opportunities for their clients based on the intersection of client needs and IBM capabilities. This app would be an effective alternative to the expensive, unscalable face-to-face workshops that IBM typically uses.

We were tasked with concepting and creating this app to help her prove to IBM that it is possible.

And we did. After only a couple months of being used by (XX# of) sellers, results showed $53M tagged pipeline was identified and directly and solely attributed to the virtual enablement of the app.

The process for creating this app dramatically changed over the 1.2 years that I've was on this project.

STAGE ONE

Proof of concept/rapid prototyping

I was brought on to this project after a rigorous work session where VSA and the client came up with a very high-level outline of the process. We jumped straight into a high-fidelity prototype to create a proof of concept in a few months.

We began with 1 weeks sprints (!!) which required vigorous whole-team whiteboarding of broad sections of the process, speedy design comps/wires, mid-week check-in with the client (an expert seller), adapt to client feedback, while simultaneously developing.

STAGE TWO

MORE features, more true agile

After a while, both our client and our team felt like we had established the basis of the app. While development continued to build the prototype, we used an Invision prototype of the experience (50 screens) to user test with IBM sellers and executives. The response was helpful and crucial to to get the OK to continue the project and create the essential features for the alpha release of the app. These two week sprints involved design/UX working side-by-side with development to create features throughout the app in a very agile process.

STAGE THREE

User feedback & transitioning

We got into a great cadence with the team in creating features and growing the application for more users. The alpha and beta versions were put in front of users and real user feedback began to establish what was prioritized in the backlog. Some of our most anticipated pain points were verified by user testing and we were able to iterate to improve the experience of the app.

At this point, the client was ready to start taking the development in-house. So, we began transitioning our dev team off while design and UX started envisioning future work.

STAGE FOUR

Waterfall and off-shore developers

The last phase was ran more like a waterfall project. Features and work was prioritized by the business needs. The VSA team became slimmer (Me, a UXer, and product manager). We would create a POV on the prioritized feature/issue, get sign-off from the client, then design it. Hand-off to development was a much more documented process where we use Zeplin, spec notes, and a screencast to communicate with developers in China.

My role
I was able to see this app through from (almost) the beginning with great ownership and growth.

Master Sketch file

I was the only consistant designer on this project from the beginning. From phase 2 on, I saw a need for a master file to keep me and eventual teammates up to date on UI elements and the overall experience. The team could reference this file when they needed to create high fidelity wires, needed to reference an element across screens, or when having design/UX conversations.

Invision prototypes & handoff

Throughout the project, we would use InVision to present our work to the client. I was in charge of creating and walking the client through these flows for each new experience and feature. We also used a massive invision prototype to user test and present to executives at the company.

To reduce duplicative work, I initiated VSA’s use of inVision Inspect to improve handoff with devs. After the client approved the presentation, I was able to point the devs to the comps with assets, flows, and specs already baked in. When we switched to working with offshore developers, attention to detail in my comps was even more important.

Design system & component library

After we passed the scrappy phases of the project, I was able to put together and maintain a component library of reusable elements. I also sat down with the developers to start to clean up the CSS and discrepancies.

This library included branding elements, components and their various states, and grid information. This library has proven to be useful when a new designer would onboard, when we handed off to the 3rd party developers, and as the client has taken the project in-house to continue.

Design/UX QA

Near the end of each sprint, I was in charge of making sure the coded product was working and displaying correctly. Using DoneDone, I would QA for design/UX elements and provide corrections for the developers to fix.

UX Thinking

This project has let me flex my UX thinking muscles. There have been 4 different UXers on this project.. at least 2 of them I had to on-board. Near the beginning of this project I took on a very strong UX role simply because the UXers were not able to give the project as much attention or we would have to go straight from whiteboarding to designs due to time. Near the end of the project, I was able to work with some amazing dedicated UXers and soaked up their knowledge while collaborating them on UX/UI elements.

Home

First time users, see a brief onboarding slideshow before they land on the Home page. Here they can see an overview of the progress of their workspaces/teams, and jump into a workspace, and begin working in any phase of the process.

Understand ⇢ Ideate ⇢ Prioritize ⇢ Build

Understand

This first phase is meant to help ground the seller. The user can view an aggregate view of the latest data on their client. Such as win rates, spending, latest news, etc. With this information, they are asked to select goals/needs that they believe are the most important to their client to begin the enablement process.

Ideate

Filtered by the client goals previously selected, industry-specific thought-starter cards called “Sparks” are displayed in a gallery view. The user can browse, favorite, and deep-dive into extensive information of each Spark card. When the seller is inspired, by a Spark or outside source, they can create their own Idea and publish it to the team.

Prioritize

In this phase, the team can see all of the Ideas created. To get team consensus, each seller rates the Ideas based on feasibility and importance to the client. The strongest Ideas that the team agrees on begin to sort themselves to top of the list and graph.

Build

After the team promotes the strongest Ideas, they become an Opportunity Brief in the Build phase of the app. Here, the team can begin to draft the critical elements needed to engage with the client collaboratively. They do this by rating elements in a list or by collaboratively writing statements (Google Doc like) that are necessary for creating quality pipeline for IBM sellers.
